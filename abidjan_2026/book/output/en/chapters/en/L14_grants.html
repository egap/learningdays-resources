15.1 Grant as argument

15.1.1 Six-part argument

  1. Answering your research question is important
  2. It can be answered with the proposed research design
  3. The design can be executed with the plan, timeline, and budget proposed
  4. You are capable of executing the research
  5. You will use funds efficiently and responsibly and deliver all outputs
  6. Your proposal fits the funder’s goals and priorities

15.1.2 An argument to whom?

  • Typical process: individual reviews, panel discussion, budget review, final decision.
  • Write for experts and non-experts, inside and outside the funding organization.
  • The funder makes the final decision.

15.2 Core components

15.2.1 Three components

  1. Project description
  2. Budget and budget justification
  3. Personnel and available resources

Let’s look at an example call for applications.

15.2.2 1. The stakes

Evaluators ask: what is at stake in answering your question?

  • What beliefs, decisions, or policies would your findings change?
  • How is current knowledge incorrect or insufficient?

Stronger proposals generalize to other problems and contexts.

15.2.3 1. Research design

Funders ask: can your design answer the question?

  • Include everything covered this week (design, randomization, analysis, power…).
  • Strong designs incorporate lessons from pilots or prior research.

Follow the guidelines (page/word limits, etc.).

15.2.4 1. Timeline

Funders ask: is the plan feasible?

  • Specific tasks and deliverables with dates.
  • Allow time for funding decisions and money transfer — be realistic.

Example spreadsheet — call for applications

15.2.6 2. Budget (1)

  • Itemized budget: cost per unit, number of units, total
    • Helps ensure nothing is missing; use funds efficiently
  • Budget justification: short description of each line
  • Note any co-funding or other resources

15.2.7 2. Budget (2)

Funders ask: efficient and responsible use of funds?

  • Adding a final phase to a project with existing resources is attractive.
  • Is there a better or less expensive way to do the study?

Example budget spreadsheet

15.2.9 3. Personnel

  • Required: research team members and CVs/resumés

Funders read CVs for:

  • Expertise (subject, context, statistics, local perspective…)
  • Experience (similar research, managing money and people, teamwork)

15.2.10 Recap: six-part argument

  1. Important question
  2. Answerable design
  3. Executable plan
  4. Capable team
  5. Efficient, responsible use of funds
  6. Good fit with funder priorities
